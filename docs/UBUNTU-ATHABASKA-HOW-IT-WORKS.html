Common Ground Initiative · The Whole Solution

One site. Everyone moves forward.

28 Athabaska Avenue · 516 homes · North York, Toronto — how the landowner, the non-profit operator, the governments, and residents each win from the same structure.

The Common Ground Initiative is not a sale and not a charity ask — it's a structure that lines up four parties around a single site so each one comes out ahead. The landowner keeps most of their value and stays in the upside; a non-profit owns and operates 516 homes with no capital down; governments fund it with insured and forgivable capital that earns a fiscal return; and 155 households get permanently affordable homes. Ubuntu Land Trust structures, underwrites, and coordinates the whole thing.

How it flows over 20 years
Day 1 · Close

Structure & build

  • Landowner leases the land in; draws $24.6M cash.
  • BCH + CMHC + City fund the $328M build.
  • Non-profit takes 100% ownership of the building.
  • Ubuntu LTD coordinates and underwrites.
Years 1–20 · Operate

Run & share

  • 516 homes operate; 155 held at 80% of MMR.
  • NOI ~$9.0M; one $164M CMHC mortgage at 1.14× DSCR.
  • Net cash flow split 60% landowner / 40% non-profit.
  • Mortgage pays down; equity builds.
Year 20 · Hand-off

Permanent & owned

  • Non-profit buys the land; landowner gets $34.3M.
  • Non-profit owns land + building (~$187M net equity).
  • Affordability is locked in for the long term.
  • Clean exit for the landowner.
Why it holds together
Incentives are aligned, not traded off.Each party's gain depends on the building getting built and run well — no one wins at another's expense.
The affordability earns the financing.30% at 80% MMR unlocks the CMHC MLI Select terms (higher leverage, 50-yr amortization) the whole stack relies on.
No one over-extends.Landowner deploys no cash; non-profit deploys no capital; the only amortizing debt is the insured 50% mortgage.
The land ends up where it should.With the non-profit, permanently — backing affordable homes rather than trading hands again.
